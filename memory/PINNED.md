## DigitalFire FeO Error: 81.8 vs 71.844

CONFIRMED: DigitalFire uses FeO = 81.8 g/mol, which is WRONG. Every other source (IUPAC, PubChem, CRC, Glazy) agrees on ~71.844 g/mol. The difference is 9.956 — a 13.9% error. This is likely a typo or data entry error in Tony Hansen's database. FeO = Fe(55.845) + O(15.999) = 71.844. The value 81.8 doesn't correspond to any known oxide.

Ryan built a comprehensive Molar Weight Database (molar_weights_database.json) covering 1912-2024 with multiple era sets (Stull 1912, IUPAC milestones, CRC, Glazy, DigitalFire). Key insight: historical glazes calculated with old atomic weights plot differently on modern Stull chart — points shift left (SiO₂) and down (Al₂O₃). A 1912 'glossy' glaze might appear 'matte' with 2023 weights. This enables a "Recipe Time Machine" feature.

Stull Atlas currently uses values in constants.ts that closely match IUPAC 2021/2023. The app does NOT include FeO as an OxideSymbol at all (only Fe2O3). FeO should be an RO flux, not R2O3 stabilizer like Fe2O3.

**Why it's pinned:** This is foundational research for the Stull Atlas project — the molar weight database, the DigitalFire FeO error discovery, and the multi-era calculation concept are all critical for the app's scientific credibility and future features.

## Advancing Glaze Chemistry — The Six Openings (Feb 11, 2026)

Ryan asked: what can be done to advance glaze chemistry? Six structural gaps identified:

**1. The Molar Weight Problem.** Every glaze tool silently bakes in one set of molar weights. Nobody tracks which era. A 1912 Stull recipe calculated with 1912 atomic weights plots differently than the same recipe with IUPAC 2023 values. The "Recipe Time Machine" — showing where a historical glaze *actually sat* when its creator formulated it — would be genuine new scholarship. We already built the molar weight database (molar_weights_database.json, 1912-2024, multiple era sets). This is ready to implement.

**2. The Stull Chart is a Slice, Not the Territory.** Stull mapped SiO₂:Al₂O₃ at fixed flux — a 2D slice through high-dimensional space. The chart can't see CaO vs KNaO balance, B₂O₃ effects, LOI, or cooling rate. The field treats the Stull chart like it *is* glaze chemistry instead of one projection. What's missing: a tool that lets you navigate the actual space and understand which projections are informative for which questions. This is what Stull Atlas is becoming.

**3. Firing Curves Are Invisible.** Almost no glaze software accounts for firing schedule. Heatwork (the Orton concept) is a time-temperature integral, not a peak temperature. A slow bisque and a fast glaze fire produce different results than the reverse. A thermal history model layered onto UMF would be new territory.

**4. The Empirical Data Is Scattered and Unstructured.** Glazy's total DB may be ~340K entries (per 2023 Widjono paper), but usable public glaze recipes with chemistry + photos is likely ~12K. DigitalFire has decades of testing. Individual potters have notebooks. None of it is structured for statistical analysis. Nobody has done serious clustering on the Glazy dataset — "show me all recipes that *actually fired* to glossy cone 6 oxidation, plotted in UMF space." ML on glaze outcomes is wide open but requires someone who understands both chemistry and data engineering.

**5. The Surface-Chemistry Gap.** UMF tells you about the glass. It tells you almost nothing about surface quality — the thing potters actually care about. Matteness, opacity, crystal formation, color response depend on cooling, atmosphere, layer thickness, clay body interaction, and microstructure. No computational model connects UMF to surface. A framework that even *categorizes* the mechanisms (phase separation vs crystal growth vs devitrification vs underfiring) and maps them to UMF regions would advance understanding significantly.

**6. Ground Truth Is Missing.** Ryan's patent thesis (#63/975,104). The field runs on trust — trust that DigitalFire's analysis is right, that Glazy's user-submitted data is accurate, that the material your supplier ships matches the published analysis. Nobody verifies. A culture of verification — cross-referencing supplier analyses, flagging recipes where the math doesn't close, detecting material drift — would be infrastructure the entire field needs.

**The honest summary:** Glaze chemistry has good 1920s science, decent 1990s software, and almost no 21st-century thinking. The people who know the chemistry don't build software. The people who build software don't fire kilns. Ryan is in the intersection.

**Why it's pinned:** This is the strategic roadmap for where Stull Atlas and the broader glaze chemistry work can go. These six openings define the research and engineering agenda. Every future session touching the atlas, glaze data, or ceramics tools should know these exist.
